# Project brief -- thomasson-kinds-projectibility (Piece 2, for philosophers)

## Genesis

Companion to the linguistics review `../thomasson-sfl-review/`. Both grew from a reading session on Thomasson's trilogy: *Ontology Made Easy* (2014), *Norms and Necessity* (2020), and *Rethinking Metaphysics* (2025), read against Brett's projectibility-first, field-relative account of kinds. Dupré's *The Disorder of Things* (1993) is now the bridge source for pluralist, anti-essentialist realism. This is the philosophy-facing position article.

## Audience and genre

Philosophers (easy ontology / conceptual engineering / natural kinds). A position article, not a review. It advances the positive program, using Thomasson as the foil and partial ally.

## Thesis (three moves)

1. **The SFL dependency is dispensable.** Her deflationary results ride on easy ontology plus framework-neutral functional facts; the function-in-syntax framing does no inferential work it cannot get elsewhere, and she half-concedes this (ch 9). (Detail lives in Piece 1; here it is one section, pitched to philosophers.)
2. **The opponent she does not meet.** She engages natural kinds mainly on the Kripke-Putnam essentialist axis she wants to deflate. Across the three-book sequence searched so far, Boyd, Khalidi, Dupré, Magnus, and Slater are absent. The missing opponent is not a crude essentialist realism, but the anti-essentialist realist tradition that treats kinds through projectibility, causal structure, plural classification, and support for induction.
3. **The constructive alternative.** A field-relative, projectibility-first account delivers the function-first payoff without the function-in-syntax commitment. The current version should use the vocabulary of projectibility profiles, support grades, and demotion conditions from Brett's recent kinds papers, not the older shortcut of calling every useful cluster an HPC.

## The convergence, stated honestly

Thomasson's function-first move is, structurally, Brett's field-relativity: a category earns standing within a field's projective economy; there is no field-neutral pre-given object (aggregating across fields is a methodological move, not the discovery of a unity). The article should grant this near-complete agreement rather than manufacture rivalry. Other shared ground: both reject Sider-style structure and the layered picture of reality; both are pluralist; both are anti-essentialist.

The new source-lockdown strengthens the convergence. OME shows that Thomasson can already handle world-deferential application conditions for natural-kind terms: the platypus example explicitly allows deference to the world and to experts. RM's fish discussion similarly treats biological concepts as revisable in light of empirical discovery and the inferential functions they serve. The article therefore should not say that Thomasson cannot accommodate natural kinds. The claim is narrower and stronger: she does not engage the anti-essentialist realist tradition in which projectibility, support grades, perturbation, and demotion supply a more disciplined account of why some function-relative categories earn standing.

## The residual disagreement (locate it precisely)

Not "is projection purpose-relative" (both say yes). The remaining inch: Brett wants support structure to be objective independent of any field's purpose; Thomasson's missing-value / is-ought point says the world constrains but does not mandate which categories we should use. Brett's strongest counter: projectibility checks make *falsifiable perturbation predictions*, which are world-answerable in a way "useful for our purposes" is not. The honest question for the article: does that falsifiability buy more mind-independence than her empirical-constraint-within-function already grants? State it as a live question, not a knockdown.

## Framing discipline

- Projectibility-first, not HPC-first. Projectibility is the criterion (Goodman). Whatever sustains the clustering (corrective feedback, frozen accident, network position, isolation) is downstream, not criterial. Do not lead with mechanism.
- Goodman, not Boyd, does the headline work. Boyd/Khalidi enter as the realist tradition Thomasson omits, not as the foundation of the view.
- Dupré enters as an ally and bridge, not as the final architecture. He gives pluralist realism and goal-relative classification; Brett's newer work adds support grades, field discipline, perturbation tests, and demotion rules.
- Use the latest internal framework: category/profile/kind-claim distinction; stable profile vs network order vs maintenance vs corrective control; field as a stabilized practice or inquiry regime, not an arbitrary purpose.

## Proposed structure

1. Introduction: function-first convergence, the missing anti-essentialist realist opponent, and the residual question about projectibility.
2. Thomasson's function-first account: OME application conditions, NN modal normativism, RM conceptual engineering.
3. The dispensable SFL dependency: useful for her presentation, not required for the philosophical result.
4. The opponent she does not meet: Goodmanian projectibility, Dupré's promiscuous realism, Boyd/Khalidi/Magnus/Slater as the omitted anti-essentialist realist tradition.
5. Projectibility profiles and field-relative kinds: the positive alternative, with support grades and demotion conditions.
6. The residual disagreement: purpose-relativity, missing value, and whether falsifiable perturbation predictions buy more mind-independence.
7. Conclusion.

## Source anchors now available

- Thomasson 2014, pp. 93--95: application conditions as rules of use; p. 94: world/expert deference for natural-kind terms.
- Thomasson 2014, pp. 327--329: conceptual analysis, conceptual engineering, and linguistic-functional anthropology after easy ontology.
- Thomasson 2020, pp. 121--123: causal/nomological modality and scientific laws as empirical-inference norms; natural-kind concepts justified by predictive/explanatory generalizations.
- Thomasson 2025, pp. 216--217: conceptual engineering constrained by worldly facts; fish and biological kind concepts.
- Dupré 1993, pp. 5--7, 57--58, 235, 262, 279 n. 20: goal-relative classification, promiscuous realism, species as individuals or modest kinds depending on context, and only passing use of Goodmanian projectibility.
