\begin{abstract}
Amie Thomasson's metaphysics is function-first. Existence and modal questions turn on rules of use for terms and claims; category questions turn on the roles concepts and categories play in discourse and inquiry. I argue that this program doesn't depend on systemic functional linguistics. Its needed premise is functional pluralism about discourse, and ordinary linguistic practice can be treated as one field among others, not a neutral court over metaphysics.

Thomasson leaves anti-essentialist projectibility realism unaddressed. Projectibility-first realism accepts her pluralism while adding support grades, failure conditions, and demotion rules. Once a field and projection are specified, category profiles can be better or worse supported by the world. That support is neither a field-neutral essence nor reducible to conceptual usefulness.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} conceptual engineering; natural kinds; projectibility; deflationism; easy ontology; modal normativism

\section{Introduction}

Thomasson's work gives metaphysics a functional turn. Existence questions are handled through application conditions for terms \citep{thomasson2014ontology}. Metaphysical modal claims express object-language rules for what can be said, inferred, and challenged in modal discourse, rather than descriptions of modal features of reality \citep{thomasson2020norms}. Conceptual engineering becomes central once we ask which concepts and categories should be retained, revised, or introduced \citep{thomasson2025rethink}. These claims don't eliminate metaphysics. They redirect it.

Here \term{function} means role in a practice, not causal mechanism, biological purpose, or syntactic slot. Four levels need to be kept distinct. At the semantic and conceptual level, terms have rules for correct application and inference. At the discourse level, an expression or claim can mark a commitment, license a contrast, repair disagreement, or organize inquiry.

At the field-inferential level, categories license projections and answer questions. At the testing level, those projections can be preserved, weakened, or defeated. The paper uses \term{function} across use, discourse, and inquiry, not as a claim about linguistic architecture.

A small linguistic example keeps the levels apart. \mention{London} can be treated as a \term{proper name} when the question concerns reference, opacity, or identity, and as a \term{proper noun} when the question concerns distribution, determiner resistance, agreement, or modification. The same string can have a referential role and a syntactic profile, but one isn't read off from the other.

A field-relative view also changes how ordinary discourse enters the argument. By a \term{field}, I mean a stabilized practice or inquiry with its own questions, uptake patterns, competence norms, repair routines, and defeaters. Ordinary linguistic practice can be treated as one such field. Metaphysics is another, including hard ontology understood as technical inquiry into what exists and how it's structured. Thomasson often lets ordinary discourse discipline metaphysical practice. That may be right when metaphysicians claim to use ordinary terms, but it isn't automatic.

This paper accepts Thomasson's redirection. Its first claim is that her function-first metaphysics needs less linguistic machinery than she sometimes suggests. The philosophical result depends on a general point: different parts of discourse have different semantic, inferential, and sorting roles, and metaphysics often goes wrong by treating all of them as attempts to describe the world's basic inventory.

Natural kinds supply the second claim. Thomasson is at her strongest when addressing Kripke-Putnam essentialism. She has the resources to resist the thought that metaphysical inquiry discovers a privileged structure that any adequate category scheme has to mirror. But a large anti-essentialist tradition treats kinds through projectibility, causal support, plural categorization, and defeasible inductive use. That tradition is the unaddressed realist alternative.

I defend a field-relative account of kind claims. A category has standing within a field when membership in it supports reliable projections: observing some features licenses expectations about others in ways that survive appropriate tests \citep{Goodman1955}. A kind claim then says that this standing is strong enough for kindhood in that field. This account grants Thomasson's anti-essentialism and functional pluralism. It denies that those concessions settle the realist question. The point isn't to choose between realism and pluralism. It's to distinguish conceptual-engineering pluralism from projectibility-constrained pluralism.

\section{Thomasson's function-first metaphysics}

In \emph{Ontology Made Easy}, Thomasson explains existence questions through application conditions. These are rules for competent use in discourse rather than definitions speakers have to state. Nor are they merely evidence conditions. They specify when applying a term would be correct, not simply when we'd be warranted in thinking the term applies \citep[93--95]{thomasson2014ontology}.

This machinery already accommodates more empirical deference than a simple conventionalist reading would suggest. Thomasson's \mention{platypus} example allows natural-kind terms to defer to empirical facts and to experts when biological distinctions are at issue \citep[94]{thomasson2014ontology}.

That doesn't mean a technical category automatically sets the standards for everyday discourse in every setting. It means that some ordinary natural-kind uses already include a norm of deference when biological correctness is at issue. So the objection can't be that Thomasson has no room for natural-kind terms, causal discovery, or expert correction.

\emph{Norms and Necessity} extends the same functional strategy to modal discourse. For metaphysical modal discourse, modal claims express rules of use rather than tracking independent modal features. Thomasson also separates that claim from causal, nomological, dispositional, and empirical counterfactual claims. At the field-inferential level, scientific laws can guide empirical inference, and natural-kind categories can be assessed by how well they support predictive and explanatory generalizations \citep[121--123]{thomasson2020norms}.

\emph{Rethinking Metaphysics} makes the positive program explicit. Conceptual engineering isn't arbitrary when it responds to empirical, technological, and social constraints. Thomasson's discussion of \mention{fish} is especially important. Biological concepts can change as empirical discoveries and theoretical purposes change; the resulting categories can be assessed by how well they support generalization and prediction \citep[216--217]{thomasson2025rethink}.

These passages create the central convergence. Thomasson already has an empirically constrained account of concept and category choice, responsive to the roles concepts play in use, discourse, inquiry, and revision. The question is whether that account exhausts what a realist projectibility theory wants from kinds.

The projectibility-first account should keep that lesson without making the route one-way. Kind inquiry can start from categories already in use or from a discovered profile of co-varying properties. What it can't start from is a field-neutral inventory of essences. In either direction, the kind claim earns standing only when the category and profile support projections within a field.

Projectibility isn't just another purpose a concept might serve. It's a way of testing whether a category's success outruns our endorsement of it. A projectible category licenses expectations that can be preserved, weakened, or defeated when conditions change. That gives kind claims a world-answerable dimension even when they remain plural and field-relative.

\section{The dispensable Hallidayan dependency}

\emph{Rethinking Metaphysics} gives systemic functional linguistics a constructive role. Thomasson uses it to identify what linguistic forms do, and especially to explain why noun-term recastings are useful rather than ontologically suspect \citep[133--160]{thomasson2025rethink}.

Her central cases are simple. From \mention{The barn is red}, speakers can move to \mention{the barn has} [\mention{the property of redness}]; from \mention{there are two cups on the table}, they can move to [\mention{the number of cups on the table}] \mention{is two}. SFL helps explain why these shifts aren't idle ontological inflation: the noun-term versions let speakers refer back, qualify, quantify, generalize, and build theory with the recast material \citep[154--156, 169--179]{thomasson2025rethink}.

Systemic functional linguistics is a Hallidayan grammatical framework that treats linguistic form in relation to what language lets speakers do in social use. In that framework, discourse roles aren't a later pragmatic add-on to grammar. They belong inside the architecture of grammatical description.

That use of SFL motivates the dependency question. If Thomasson's metaphysical result depends on the Hallidayan placement of discourse roles inside grammar, then a general point about discourse roles has been tied to one grammatical architecture. \textcite{reynolds2026thomassonSFLReview} develops the linguistic version of the extraction argument: SFL supplies a useful diagnostic vocabulary, but the relevant discourse-role insight can be stated without adopting Hallidayan grammatical architecture. This paper asks what follows for kinds once that detachment is granted.

Only a modest premise is needed: expressions and claims can have different discourse jobs. Easy ontology and modal normativism already use role-sensitive resources, and inferentialist, pragmatist, or conceptual engineering vocabularies can state the same point. The barn and cup examples need the easy-ontology machinery plus an account of why the recast formulations are worth having; they don't need the stronger grammar.

For this paper, the detachment claim is limited. I am not arguing that the linguistic framing is useless. It may help Thomasson organize the view and connect it to a broader account of discourse. The claim is narrower: the metaphysical payoff comes from function-first pluralism, not from function-in-syntax.

\section{The unaddressed realist alternative}

Thomasson's main natural-kind focus is the essentialist tradition associated with Kripke and Putnam \citep{Kripke1980,Putnam1975Meaning}. That focus is important, but it isn't the whole debate. The anti-essentialist projectibility tradition isn't treated as the central realist alternative.

The omission matters because the anti-essentialist realist tradition grants much of what Thomasson wants. Goodman made projectibility central to induction: not every true predicate supports reliable projection \citep{Goodman1955}. His point can be put without the technical details of \mention{grue}: two predicates can fit all observed cases while only one supports new expectations. The difference lies in projectibility, not in past truth alone.

Dupré rejects the idea that objects generally have one context-independent natural kind and defends a pluralist realism on which overlapping categories can be equally real \citep[5--7, 57--58, 262]{dupre1993disorder}. Boyd's homeostatic-property-cluster account, Khalidi's causal-network account, Magnus's pluralist critique of HPC reduction, and Slater's \term{natural kindness} all weaken or reject essentialism while retaining a realist role for kinds \citep{boyd1991,boyd1999,khalidi2013,khalidi2018nodes,magnus2012scientific,slater2015}.

Boyd gives the clearest connection to Thomasson. On his accommodationist view, the reality of a kind turns on whether reference to it contributes, within a disciplinary setting, to reliable inference and accommodation to causal structure. Different species concepts can answer to different explanatory projects without becoming merely unreal or arbitrary \citep[169--176]{boyd1999}. Khalidi makes the parallel point in causal-network terms. Categories needn't be mind-independent; they need to serve epistemic purposes by tracking causal patterns and supporting projectible inferences \citep[202--203]{khalidi2013}.

Species concepts make the point concrete. In an ecosystem-oriented inquiry, a field biologist may care about organisms that occupy the same niche, compete for the same resources, host the same parasites, or play the same trophic role. That category should project interaction effects: if one population is removed, which relations in the system change? In a genetic inquiry, the central projections concern gene flow, reproductive isolation, ancestry, and hybridization. Two populations can count together as species for ecological purposes and apart for genetic purposes, or vice versa, without either result being arbitrary.

Dupré still matters because he states the pluralist lesson bluntly. Category choice depends on the goals of inquiry, but the resulting kinds needn't be unreal. Species may be treated as individuals in some contexts and as modest kinds in others \citep[235]{dupre1993disorder}. He uses Goodmanian projectibility only in passing \citep[279 n.~20]{dupre1993disorder}, so he doesn't provide the full apparatus of support grades, demotion conditions, and field discipline that a projectibility-first account needs.

\section{Projectibility-first, field-relative kinds}

A projectibility-first account begins with the projection to be licensed. A category earns standing when membership in it supports reliable expectations about other features, behaviours, failure modes, or intervention outcomes. The first question isn't what essence all members share, but what inferences the category licenses, under what conditions, and with what known breakdown points.

This requires three distinctions. First, a category is a sorting device. Second, a profile is the pattern of properties, relations, or behaviours that makes the sorting projectible. Third, a kind claim says that the category earns standing by tracking that profile well enough for some field of inquiry or practice. Confusing these three levels makes weak categories look like well-supported kinds.

Field-relativity here isn't whim-relativity. Fields have measurement routines, authorities, records, and defeaters. Field-relative kind claims can be answerable to the world because their projections can fail. Categories can also be demoted: a category may remain useful for one task while losing standing for another.

The apparatus has three parts. Support grades concern the reliability, scope, and counterfactual robustness of the projections a category licenses. Failure conditions specify what would count as breakdown rather than noise: anomalous cases, field changes, intervention effects, or loss of explanatory integration. Demotion rules specify when a category remains useful for sorting or communication while losing kind-standing for a particular inquiry.

Consider \mention{weed}. In gardening or municipal maintenance, dandelion, crabgrass, and knotweed can belong together because the category projects intervention outcomes: spread, competition with intended plants, removal costs, and regrowth. In botany, the same category has little standing if the question is ancestry or morphology. It remains practically useful while losing botanical kind-standing.

The \mention{fish} case shows the distinction. In ordinary food and market practices, \mention{fish} can be a useful category because it supports expectations about handling, preparation, and labelling. In phylogenetic systematics, the same category can be demoted because it doesn't track the ancestry relations that field asks about. In ecological work, a narrower category built around aquatic life may still support expectations about habitat, trophic role, or exposure. The word alone doesn't settle the kind claim; the field and projection do.

The \mention{platypus} example shows the other direction. Thomasson's world-deferential application conditions leave room for experts to correct ordinary applications when biological correctness is at issue. A person can know how to use \mention{platypus} without knowing which traits carry the biological projections. Mammalogy can still treat the term as tied to a profile that supports expectations about related traits and explains why some apparent counterexamples are errors, immature cases, or abnormal cases. That's stronger than a loose grouping, but still not a field-neutral essence.

If HPC is read as a claim about stabilizing mechanisms alone, it can obscure this point. This realism is broader than homeostatic mechanism realism. A stable profile, a network position, a sustaining mechanism, and corrective control can come apart. Some projectible profiles are control-secured. Others draw support from history, isolation, institutional uptake, or background network structure. Homeostatic stabilization is one source of support, not the criterion for kindhood.

\section{The residual disagreement}

The disagreement with Thomasson should be stated narrowly. It isn't about whether purposes matter, or whether empirical facts constrain concept and category choice. Thomasson's own discussion of \mention{fish} shows that conceptual engineering can be worldly and non-arbitrary \citep[216--217]{thomasson2025rethink}.

A priority worry remains. Ordinary linguistic practice can appear to occupy the unmarked position, while hard ontology is treated as suspect because it departs from everyday discourse. But hard ontology is also a field, with its own vocabulary, questions, and internal standards. Its distance from ordinary discourse isn't by itself a defect. The defect would be failure to license projections, explanations, or disciplined contrasts that ordinary discourse-practice can't supply.

This standard gives hard ontology no free pass. If a metaphysician uses \mention{table} to make an ordinary existence claim, ordinary application conditions constrain the claim. If she introduces \mention{mereological fusion} or \mention{simples arranged as a table}, ordinary discourse no longer settles the issue. The technical vocabulary then has to earn its keep by explaining composition, persistence, or counting contrasts that ordinary furniture talk doesn't supply.

Nor is the worry that Thomasson preserves ordinary discourse against revision. Her conceptual engineering is explicitly revisionary. The issue is cross-field transfer. Once these practices are treated as fields of discourse and inquiry, usefulness in one doesn't by itself settle standing in another. A category can be useful, even indispensable, without yet earning kind-standing for a field-relative inquiry.

Projectibility may still add a realist constraint beyond empirically informed usefulness. A projectibility profile licenses defeasible expectations and tests: perturb a stabilizing relation, change a field condition, or move the category to a new setting, and predictive reliability should degrade in determinate ways. These aren't merely reports that a category serves our purposes. The world answers them by preserving or defeating the projected inferences.

Thomasson can respond that this still doesn't identify a field-neutral structure that mandates one correct category scheme. That response is right. The projectibility-first account needs only the weaker realist claim that, once a field and projection are specified, the world can make some category profiles better supported than others.

\section{Conclusion}

Thomasson's function-first metaphysics should be accepted in its strongest form. It redirects metaphysics toward rules of use, discourse roles, field-relative category choice, and conceptual revision. It also shouldn't be dismissed on the grounds that it can't handle natural-kind deference or empirical constraint. But ordinary discourse isn't a field-neutral court over every specialized practice, including metaphysics itself.

The anti-essentialist realist tradition remains largely outside her focus. Projectibility-first realism accepts pluralism, rejects essences, and treats kind claims as field-relative. It then adds a projectibility discipline: support grades, failure conditions, and demotion rules. Thomasson's account hasn't yet made that extra discipline unnecessary.

Projectibility-first realism doesn't ask for a privileged category scheme fixed independently of inquiry. It asks what follows once a field, a category, and a projection are specified. Some categories then earn more than usefulness: they support defeasible expectations, explain failures, and generate tests that can be preserved or defeated by the world. That's a realist constraint, but not an essentialist one.
