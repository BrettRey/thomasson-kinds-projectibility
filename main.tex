\begin{abstract}
Amie Thomasson's metaphysics is function-first: existence, modal, and category questions are addressed by asking what our terms and concepts do. I argue that this program does not depend on systemic-functional linguistics and that it leaves a realist opponent unmet. Thomasson engages natural kinds through Kripke-Putnam essentialism, not the anti-essentialist tradition built around projectibility, plural classification, and support for induction. A projectibility-first account can accept her functional pluralism while adding support grades, field discipline, and demotion conditions. The remaining issue is whether falsifiable perturbation predictions give field-relative kinds more world-answerability than her constrained conceptual engineering already grants.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} conceptual engineering; natural kinds; projectibility; deflationism; easy ontology; modal normativism

\section{Introduction}

Thomasson's work gives metaphysics a functional turn. Existence questions are handled through the application conditions of terms \citep{thomasson2014ontology}. Metaphysical modal claims are treated as object-language expressions of semantic rules rather than as descriptions of modal features of reality \citep{thomasson2020norms}. Conceptual engineering becomes a central task for metaphysics once we ask which concepts and categories should be retained, revised, or introduced \citep{thomasson2025rethink}. These claims do not eliminate metaphysics. They redirect it.

This paper accepts that redirection. Its first claim is that Thomasson's function-first metaphysics needs less linguistic machinery than she sometimes suggests. The philosophical result does not depend on systemic-functional linguistics. It depends on a more general point: different parts of discourse serve different functions, and metaphysics often goes wrong by treating all of them as attempts to describe deep structure.

The second claim is about natural kinds. Thomasson is at her strongest against essentialist realism. She has the resources to resist the thought that metaphysics discovers a privileged structure that our best terms must mirror. But that is not the only realist view of kinds. A large anti-essentialist tradition treats kinds through projectibility, causal support, plural classification, and defeasible inductive use. That is the opponent she does not meet.

The constructive alternative is a field-relative account of kinds. A category earns standing within a field when it supports reliable projections in that field: when observing some features licenses us to expect others in ways that survive appropriate tests \citep{Goodman1955}. This account grants Thomasson's anti-essentialism and her functional pluralism. It denies that those concessions settle the realist question. Some function-relative categories support disciplined projections, explain their failures, and generate falsifiable perturbation predictions. Others do not.

\section{Thomasson's function-first metaphysics}

In \emph{Ontology Made Easy}, Thomasson explains existence questions through application conditions. These are semantic rules of use, not definitions speakers must be able to state. They are also not merely evidence conditions. They specify when it would be correct to apply a term, not simply when we would be warranted in thinking the term applies \citep[93--95]{thomasson2014ontology}.

This machinery already accommodates more empirical deference than a simple conventionalist reading would suggest. Thomasson's \mention{platypus} example explicitly allows natural-kind terms to have world-deferential application conditions. Such terms can defer to the world, and to experts with specialized knowledge of the world, to determine what distinguishes one biological kind from another \citep[94]{thomasson2014ontology}. So the objection cannot be that Thomasson has no room for natural-kind terms, causal discovery, or expert deference.

\emph{Norms and Necessity} extends the same functional strategy to modal discourse. Metaphysical modal claims express semantic rules rather than tracking independent modal features. But Thomasson also distinguishes this project from causal, nomological, dispositional, and empirical counterfactual claims. Scientific laws can be treated as norms for empirical inference, and natural-kind concepts can be justified when they support predictive and explanatory generalizations \citep[121--123]{thomasson2020norms}.

\emph{Rethinking Metaphysics} makes the positive program explicit. Conceptual engineering is not arbitrary when it responds to empirical, technological, and social constraints. Thomasson's discussion of \mention{fish} is especially important. Biological concepts can change as empirical discoveries and theoretical purposes change, and their revision can be assessed by how well they support generalization and prediction \citep[216--217]{thomasson2025rethink}.

These passages create the central convergence. Thomasson already has a function-relative, empirically constrained account of category choice. The question is whether that account exhausts what a realist projectibility theory wants from kinds.

\section{The dispensable systemic-functional dependency}

The function-first conclusion does not require systemic-functional linguistics. A philosopher can reach the same result from easy ontology, modal normativism, inferentialism, pragmatism, or conceptual engineering. The needed premise is functional pluralism about discourse: not every term or sentence has the job of naming or describing an entity, property, or deep metaphysical structure.

That point matters because the systemic-functional apparatus can distract from the stronger philosophical result. If the argument depends on a contested linguistic theory, critics can treat it as hostage to that theory. But Thomasson's own examples do not require that dependency. Application conditions, world-deferential rules, non-descriptive modal vocabulary, and pragmatic conceptual engineering can all be stated without taking on the additional commitments of systemic-functional grammar.

For this paper, the point is limited. I am not arguing that the linguistic framing is useless. It may help Thomasson organize the view and connect it to a broader account of discourse. The claim is only that the metaphysical payoff comes from function-first pluralism, not from function-in-syntax.

\section{The opponent she does not meet}

Thomasson engages natural kinds primarily through the essentialist tradition associated with Kripke and Putnam \citep{Kripke1980,Putnam1975Meaning}. That is an important target, but it is not the whole debate. Full-text searches of the three books used for this draft found no occurrences of Boyd, Khalidi, Dupré, or Magnus, and no use of \term{projectibility} as a term of art.\footnote{The search record is in \texttt{notes/source-verification.md}. The absence claim should still be checked against legitimate print indexes before submission.}

The omission matters because the anti-essentialist realist tradition grants much of what Thomasson wants. Goodman made projectibility central to induction: not every true predicate supports reliable projection \citep{Goodman1955}. Dupré rejects the idea that objects generally have one context-independent natural kind and defends a pluralist realism on which overlapping classifications can be equally real \citep[5--7, 57--58, 262]{dupre1993disorder}. Boyd's homeostatic-property-cluster account, Khalidi's causal-network account, Magnus's pluralist critique of HPC reduction, and Slater's \term{natural kindness} all weaken or reject essentialism while retaining a realist role for kinds \citep{boyd1991,boyd1999,khalidi2013,khalidi2018nodes,magnus2012scientific,slater2015}.

Dupré is the most direct bridge to Thomasson. He says classification depends on the goals of inquiry, but he does not infer that the resulting kinds are unreal. Species may be treated as individuals in some contexts and as modest kinds in others \citep[235]{dupre1993disorder}. That is close to Thomasson's function-relative category choice. It is also close to a field-relative account of kinds.

Still, Dupré is only a bridge. He uses Goodmanian projectibility in passing \citep[279 n.~20]{dupre1993disorder}. He does not provide the full apparatus of support grades, demotion conditions, and field discipline that a projectibility-first account needs.

\section{Projectibility-first, field-relative kinds}

A projectibility-first account begins with the projection to be licensed. A category earns standing when membership in it supports reliable expectations about other features, behaviours, failures, or interventions. The first question is not what essence all members share. It is what inferences the category licenses, under what conditions, and with what known failure modes.

This requires three distinctions. First, a category is a sorting device. Second, a profile is the pattern of properties, relations, or behaviours that makes the sorting projectible. Third, a kind claim says that the category earns standing by tracking that profile well enough for some field of inquiry or practice. Confusing these three levels makes weak categories look stronger than they are.

The account is field-relative, but not whim-relative. A field is a stabilized practice or inquiry regime with its own questions, measurement routines, uptake patterns, authorities, records, and defeaters. Field-relative categories can be answerable to the world because their projections can fail. They can also be demoted: a category may remain useful for one task while losing standing for another.

This is where the account departs from older HPC-first formulations. A stable profile, a network position, a sustaining mechanism, and corrective control can come apart. Some projectible kinds are control-secured. Others are stable through history, isolation, institutional uptake, or background network structure. Homeostasis is one support grade, not the criterion for kindhood.

\section{The residual disagreement}

The disagreement with Thomasson should be stated narrowly. It is not about whether purposes matter. They do. It is not about whether empirical facts constrain conceptual choice. They do. Thomasson's own discussion of \mention{fish} shows that conceptual engineering can be worldly and non-arbitrary \citep[216--217]{thomasson2025rethink}.

The remaining question is whether projectibility adds a realist constraint beyond empirically informed usefulness. A projectibility profile licenses defeasible expectations. It also yields tests: perturb a stabilizing relation, change a field condition, or move the category to a new setting, and the projection should degrade in predictable ways. These are not merely reports that a category serves our purposes. They are world-answerable claims about where the category's inferential authority holds and where it fails.

Thomasson can respond that this still does not identify a field-neutral structure that mandates one correct category scheme. That response is right. The projectibility-first account does not need such a structure. It needs only the weaker realist claim that, once a field and projection are specified, the world can make some category profiles better supported than others.

\section{Conclusion}

Thomasson's function-first metaphysics should be accepted in its strongest form. It does not need systemic-functional linguistics to redirect metaphysics toward the functions of our terms, concepts, and categories. It also should not be rejected by pretending that it cannot handle natural-kind deference or empirical constraint.

The better objection is that the anti-essentialist realist tradition remains largely outside her target. Projectibility-first realism accepts pluralism, rejects essences, and treats categories as field-relative. It then asks for support grades, failure conditions, and demotion rules. That is the extra discipline Thomasson's account has not yet displaced.
